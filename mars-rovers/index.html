Exploring Mars, One Rover at a Time

NASA’s Mars Rover Program has sent robotic explorers across the surface of the Red Planet to uncover clues about its climate, geology, and potential for life. These missions not only expand our understanding of Mars but also pave the way for future human exploration. Two of the most advanced rovers—Curiosity and Perseverance—continue to revolutionize planetary science.

Curiosity Rover

Curiosity, part of the Mars Science Laboratory mission, landed in Gale Crater in 2012. Its main goal is to determine whether Mars ever had the environmental conditions to support microbial life. Equipped with a powerful set of instruments, Curiosity has uncovered evidence of ancient water, organic molecules, and a complex Martian climate history.

Perseverance Rover

Perseverance, which landed in Jezero Crater in 2021, is on a mission to search for signs of ancient life and collect samples for future return to Earth. It’s testing new technology like oxygen production and has even deployed the Ingenuity helicopter—the first aircraft to fly on another planet.
